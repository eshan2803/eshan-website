California's Clean Energy Transformation

A Data-Driven Analysis of Grid Evolution from 2020 to 2025

Key Findings at a Glance

  • Clean Energy: California's grid reached 62% clean energy by 2025 (when imports are properly classified)
  • Natural Gas: Gas generation declined by 26% from 2020 to 2025, dropping from 32% to 24% of supply
  • Battery Storage: Grew from near-zero to 11 GW capacity, reaching up to 65% of peak demand on some days
  • Renewable Penetration: Hours exceeding 100% renewable generation increased dramatically, with some days achieving 20+ hours of 100%+ clean energy
  • Negative Prices: Battery storage curtailed negative pricing events by 2025, after they peaked in 2024
  • Price Impacts: Battery deployment drove significant price cannibalization, with ancillary services and energy arbitrage revenues declining

1. Understanding Generator Types

Before diving into California's transformation, we must understand the fundamental characteristics of different electricity generators. Two key metrics define generator behavior:

  • Ramp Rate: How quickly a generator can change output (MW/hour)
  • Capacity Factor: Average output as a percentage of maximum capacity

1.1 Ramp Rate Distributions

📊 Methodology: Ramp Rate Calculation

Data Source: EIA-930 hourly generation data (2020-2025)

Calculation:

  • For each generator type, calculate hourly power changes: ΔP = P(t+1) - P(t)
  • Ramp rate = |ΔP| in MW per hour (absolute value)
  • Plot distribution as violin/density plot showing the full range of ramp rates
  • Each vertical spread shows the probability density of different ramp magnitudes

🔍 Key Insights

  • Solar exhibits the highest ramp rates (up to 10,000+ MW/hour) due to sunrise/sunset transitions and cloud cover
  • Batteries show extremely high ramp capability (up to 6,000 MW/hour), making them ideal for grid balancing
  • Natural Gas provides moderate flexibility (typically 1,000-3,000 MW/hour), crucial for following load and renewable variability
  • Nuclear and Hydro show minimal ramping, with most changes under 500 MW/hour, operating primarily as baseload resources
  • Wind shows moderate ramp rates (1,000-3,000 MW/hour) due to weather front movements

1.2 Ramp Rates vs. LMP

🔍 Key Insights

  • High ramp rate events correlate with elevated electricity prices (LMP), as the grid must dispatch flexible resources quickly
  • Solar ramps during morning and evening transitions create price spikes when gas generators must ramp up
  • Battery ramps show strong correlation with price volatility, as batteries respond to arbitrage opportunities

1.3 Capacity Factors by Season

📊 Methodology: Capacity Factor Calculation

Data Source: CAISO fuel-source data + EIA capacity data

Calculation:

  • Capacity Factor = (Actual Generation) / (Maximum Capacity × Time Period)
  • Calculated for each 5-minute interval, then averaged by season
  • Maximum capacity estimated from observed peak generation over multiple years

🔍 Key Insights

  • Nuclear operates at ~90% capacity factor year-round (baseload)
  • Solar averages 25% capacity factor, with summer peak around 30% (daylight hours only)
  • Wind shows ~35% capacity factor, with higher performance in spring/fall
  • Natural Gas operates at 40-60% capacity, varying seasonally to follow demand
  • Large Hydro shows seasonal variation (40-60%), driven by snowmelt and reservoir management

1.4 Capacity Factors vs. LMP

🔍 Key Insights

  • Higher natural gas capacity factors coincide with higher prices, indicating gas dispatch during peak demand
  • Solar capacity factor peaks (midday) often correspond with lower prices due to abundant clean energy
  • This price-capacity relationship drives battery arbitrage economics

2. Solar's Impact: The Duck Curve Phenomenon

As solar capacity exploded in California, the grid began exhibiting a distinctive daily pattern known as the "duck curve." This shape emerges from the interaction between demand, solar generation, and the resulting "net load" (demand minus renewables) that conventional generators must meet.

2.1 Duck Curve Evolution: April 2020-2025

📊 Methodology: Duck Curve Analysis

Data Source: CAISO 5-minute fuel-source data for April each year

Process:

  • Gross Demand = Sum of all generation sources, excluding battery charging
  • Solar Generation = Solar column from CAISO data
  • Battery Charging = Negative battery values (load component)
  • Net Load = Gross Demand - Solar + Battery Charging
  • Calculated hourly averages across all April days for each year

Why April? Spring months showcase the duck curve most dramatically due to moderate demand and high solar generation.

🔍 Key Insights: The Deepening Duck

  • 2020-2023: Duck curve "belly" deepened each year as solar capacity grew. Net load dropped from ~20 GW to ~12 GW at midday.
  • Ramp Challenge: Evening ramp steepened dramatically. Net load climbs from 12 GW (3pm) to 28 GW (7pm) = 16 GW in 4 hours (4,000 MW/hour ramp requirement)
  • Battery Impact (2024-2025): Purple shaded area shows battery charging during solar surplus hours, storing 2-4 GW. This:
    • Absorbs excess solar (reducing negative prices)
    • Smooths the evening ramp by discharging during sunset
  • Net Load Transformation: By 2025, the duck curve begins to flatten as batteries fill the belly and shave the neck

2.2 Negative Price Events

📊 Methodology: Negative Price Analysis

Data Source: CAISO LMP price data + fuel-source generation data

Negative Price Definition: Hours when day-ahead LMP < $0/MWh

Analysis:

  • Count negative price hours per year
  • Correlate with solar generation levels
  • Track minimum price magnitudes
  • Examine seasonal distribution

🔍 Key Insights: The Battery Solution

  • 2020-2023: Negative price hours increased exponentially
    • 2020: ~500 hours with negative prices
    • 2023: ~3,000 hours with negative prices
    • Worst prices reached -$30 to -$50/MWh
  • Root Cause: Solar generation exceeded demand during spring midday, but:
    • Must-run nuclear and hydro couldn't curtail
    • Gas generators already at minimum output
    • Limited export capacity to neighbors
  • 2024 Peak: Negative price hours peaked at ~3,500 hours, the worst year
  • 2025 Reversal: Battery storage capacity reached critical mass:
    • Negative price hours dropped to ~1,500 hours (-57%)
    • Price magnitudes softened (rarely below -$20/MWh)
    • Batteries absorbed 2-4 GW of excess solar during price crashes

3. Battery Storage Revolution

Between 2020 and 2025, California deployed utility-scale battery storage at an unprecedented rate, fundamentally transforming grid operations. From negligible capacity in 2020, batteries grew to 11 GW by 2025—representing up to 65% of peak demand on some days.

3.1 Battery Capacity Growth vs. LMP

📊 Methodology: Battery Data Processing

Data Source: CAISO 5-minute battery generation/charging data

Metrics:

  • Daily Peak Battery Discharge (GW) = Maximum positive battery MW value each day ÷ 1000
  • Battery % of Demand = (Peak Battery MW / Peak Gross Demand MW) × 100
  • Color Coding: Line colored by % of demand metric (plasma colormap: purple 0% → yellow 100%)

LMP Prices: Daily peak day-ahead Locational Marginal Price

🔍 Key Insights

  • Exponential Growth: Battery discharge capacity grew from ~0.5 GW (2020) to ~11 GW (2025)
  • Line Color Evolution:
    • Early years (purple): Batteries were <5% of peak demand
    • 2024-2025 (yellow/orange): Batteries reached 40-65% of peak demand
  • Price Correlation:
    • High LMP spikes (>$100/MWh) become rarer in 2024-2025
    • Batteries smooth price volatility by providing peak capacity
  • Grid Reliability: By 2025, batteries became a critical resource during:
    • Evening peak demand (6-9pm)
    • Heat waves and reliability events
    • Unexpected generator outages

3.2 Battery Discharge vs. LMP by Year

📊 Methodology: Year-by-Year Scatter Analysis

X-axis: Daily Peak Battery Discharge (GW)

Y-axis: Daily Peak LMP ($/MWh), capped at 99th percentile for readability

Color: Battery % of Peak Demand (plasma colormap)

Each subplot shows one year's data, enabling temporal comparison of battery-price relationships.

🔍 Key Insights: Market Evolution

  • 2020-2022: Tight cluster at low GW, prices highly volatile (LMP = $20-$800/MWh)
  • 2023: Horizontal spreading begins as battery capacity grows to 2-6 GW
  • 2024-2025: Major transformation:
    • Batteries spread across 0-11 GW range
    • High price events (>$500/MWh) largely disappear
    • Yellow dots (high % of demand) indicate batteries meeting 40-65% of peak load
    • Price ceiling effect visible: LMP rarely exceeds $200/MWh even on high-discharge days
  • Arbitrage Opportunity Decline: As batteries flatten price spikes, price spreads narrow, reducing future arbitrage revenue

4. Grid Economics: Price Cannibalization

As battery capacity saturated the market, an economic phenomenon emerged: price cannibalization. Batteries eat their own lunch—their very success in smoothing prices reduces the price spreads that make them profitable.

4.1 Impact on Ancillary Services

Beyond energy arbitrage, batteries provide critical ancillary services: Regulation Up (RU), Regulation Down (RD), Spinning Reserve (SR), and Non-Spinning Reserve (NR). These services maintain grid frequency and provide backup capacity.

  • AS Prices: CAISO ancillary services price data (daily peak prices for RU, RD, SR, NR)

  • Price Scatter: Colored by month (seasonal colormap: winter blue → spring green → summer yellow → fall orange)

🔍 Key Insights: The Cannibalization Effect

  • Regulation Up (RU):
    • 2020-2022: Prices regularly spiked to $200-$300/MWh
    • 2024-2025: Prices compressed to $20-$100/MWh as batteries flood the RU market
    • Battery capacity (yellow line) grew from <1 GW to 11 GW while prices collapsed
  • Regulation Down (RD):
    • Prices dropped from $50-$150/MWh range to $10-$50/MWh
    • RD market particularly affected as batteries excel at rapid downward response
  • Spinning & Non-Spinning Reserve (SR/NR):
    • Dramatic price collapse from $200-$300/MWh peaks to <$100/MWh
    • Batteries can provide these services at near-zero marginal cost
  • Revenue Implications:
    • Early battery projects (2020-2022) achieved high AS revenues
    • By 2025, AS revenues declined 60-80% from peak levels
    • Projects increasingly rely on capacity payments and IRA tax credits for profitability
  • Winner's Curse: The first wave of batteries profited immensely, but later projects face saturated markets

5. Renewable Penetration Progress

California set aggressive targets for renewable energy penetration. By tracking hourly renewable penetration and counting hours above 100%, we can measure progress toward 24/7 clean energy.

5.1 Hourly Clean Energy Penetration (2020-2025)

📊 Methodology: Clean Energy Penetration

Data Source: CAISO 5-minute fuel-source data

Definition:

  • Clean Energy = Renewables (Solar, Wind, Geothermal, Biomass, Biogas, Small Hydro) + Nuclear + Large Hydro + Battery Discharge
  • Gross Demand = Total California consumption (all generation minus battery charging)
  • Clean Energy Penetration % = (Clean Energy MWh / Gross Demand MWh) × 100

Panel 1 Visualization:

  • Each dot = one hour of data
  • Color: Hour of day (HSV colormap: red = midnight, cyan = 6am, yellow = noon, orange = 6pm)
  • White line: Daily average penetration
  • 100% threshold: White dashed line marking self-sufficiency

Panel 2: Calendar heatmap showing daily hours ≥100% penetration (0-24 hour scale, green intensity)

🔍 Key Insights: The Path to 24/7 Clean Energy

  • Diurnal Pattern (Panel 1):
    • Yellow/green dots (midday): Regularly exceed 100% due to solar abundance
    • Blue/purple dots (night): Cluster at 20-40%, limited to wind, hydro, nuclear
    • Orange/red dots (evening): 30-60%, challenging transition period
  • Daily Average Growth (white line):
    • 2020: ~45% average clean penetration
    • 2023: ~55% average
    • 2025: ~65% average
  • 100% Hours Progress (Panel 2):
    • 2020: Very few days with any 100% hours (mostly light colors)
    • 2023: Spring/summer show 2-4 hours per day occasionally
    • 2024-2025: Best spring days achieve 8-11 hours at 100%+, with consistent 5-7 hour days
    • Record: May 24, 2025 achieved 11 consecutive hours at 100%+ clean energy
  • Seasonal Patterns:
    • Spring (March-May): Peak performance due to high solar + moderate demand
    • Summer (June-August): Good performance despite high demand due to extreme solar
    • Winter (December-February): Worst performance (short days, low solar)
  • Remaining Challenge: Achieving 100% during night hours requires:
    • Longer-duration storage (8-12 hour batteries)
    • Expanded transmission to access wind resources
    • Demand flexibility and load shifting

6. Natural Gas Decline

As renewables and batteries grew, natural gas—California's largest fossil fuel source—entered structural decline.

6.1 Natural Gas Generation Over Time

📊 Methodology: Natural Gas Analysis

Data Source: CAISO fuel-source data (Natural Gas column)

Panel 1: Hourly Natural Gas Generation

  • Y-axis: Natural gas generation in MW
  • Color: Hour of day (HSV colormap)
  • White line: Daily average gas generation

Panel 2: Daily Gas % of Gross Demand

  • Calendar heatmap showing what percentage of daily energy came from natural gas
  • Calculation: For each 5-min interval, Gas % = (Gas MW / Gross Demand MW) × 100, then averaged across the day
  • Red intensity = higher gas dependence

🔍 Key Insights: Fossil Fuel Displacement

  • Decline Trajectory:
    • 2020: 8,406 MW average daily gas generation (32.2% of demand)
    • 2021-2023: Gas increased to 9,960 MW (37.3%) as demand growth outpaced renewable additions
    • 2024: Sharp reversal—dropped to 7,447 MW (28.5%)
    • 2025: Further decline to 6,188 MW (23.8%)
    • Total reduction: -2,218 MW (-26.4%) from 2020 to 2025
  • Why the 2024-2025 Inflection?
    • Battery capacity reached critical mass (6-11 GW)
    • Batteries displaced gas during evening peak (when gas was most valuable)
    • Continued solar growth further reduced midday gas need
  • Calendar Heatmap Trends (Panel 2):
    • 2020-2023: Mostly orange/red colors (25-45% gas)
    • 2024-2025: Shift to yellow/light orange (15-30% gas)
    • Spring days: Some showing <10% gas (mostly green in heatmap)
  • Seasonal Patterns:
    • Winter: Highest gas dependence (30-40%) due to low solar and high heating demand
    • Spring: Lowest gas use (10-20%) thanks to solar abundance and moderate demand

7. Energy Mix Analysis

To understand California's true progress, we need a comprehensive view of where every MWh comes from.

7.1 Original Energy Breakdown

📊 Methodology: Energy Mix (Original)

Data Source: CAISO fuel-source data

Categories:

  • Natural Gas: CAISO in-state gas generation
  • Clean Energy: Renewables (Solar, Wind, Geothermal, Biomass, Biogas, Small Hydro) + Nuclear + Large Hydro + Battery Discharge
  • Imports: Electricity imported from neighboring states (origin unknown)

Calculation:

  • For each 5-minute interval, sum generation by category
  • Integrate to daily MWh (5-min interval = 1/12 hour)
  • Gross Demand = All generation sources minus battery charging

Clean % = Clean Energy / (Gas + Clean + Imports) × 100

🔍 Key Insights

  • Three-Way Split:
    • Natural Gas (orange): ~200 GWh/day average, declining toward 150 GWh/day by 2025
    • Clean Energy (green): ~310 GWh/day average, growing toward 400+ GWh/day
    • Imports (purple): ~105 GWh/day average, stable but uncertain composition
  • Average Clean Energy: 50.9%
  • Problem: This treats all imports as "unknown," understating California's clean energy progress
  • Reality: Most imports come from Pacific Northwest hydro and Southwest renewables, not fossil fuels

8. Import Classification Methodology

The critical question: What are we importing? Is it clean hydro from the Northwest or coal from the Southwest? The answer dramatically changes California's clean energy percentage.

8.1 Data Sources and Approach

📊 Methodology: Import Source Classification

Challenge: CAISO reports total imports but doesn't break down by fuel source.

Solution: Merit Order Dispatch Model

Data Sources:

  • EIA Interchange Data (2019-2024): Hourly import flows from Northwest (NW) and Southwest (SW) regions into CAISO
  • CEC Import Mix Data: Annual breakdown of generation sources in NW and SW export regions
    • Sources: Coal, Natural Gas, Oil, Nuclear, Large Hydro, Small Hydro, Geothermal, Solar, Wind, Biomass, Unspecified, Other
    • Reported as GWh by source and region per year

Modeling Process:

  1. Merit Order Definition: Define dispatch priority (cheapest/cleanest first)
    • Priority 1: Nuclear, Geothermal, Large Hydro, Small Hydro, Solar, Wind (must-run/zero marginal cost)
    • Priority 2: Other
    • Priority 3: Coal
    • Priority 4: Unspecified (market purchases)
    • Priority 5: Natural Gas (marginal resource)
    • Priority 6: Oil
  2. Solar Profile: Model hourly solar availability using simple sine curve (peak at 1pm, zero at night)
  3. Capacity Optimization: For each source and region:
    • Initialize capacity using default capacity factors
    • Simulate hourly dispatch following merit order and availability
    • Iteratively adjust capacity until annual generation matches CEC targets (±0.1% tolerance)
  4. Hourly Dispatch: For each hour, dispatch available generation to meet import demand:
    • Dispatch sources in merit order
    • Apply availability constraints (e.g., solar profile)
    • Calculate hourly generation by source
  5. Carbon Intensity: Calculate emissions using source-specific CI factors:
    • Coal: 1000 kg CO₂/MWh
    • Natural Gas: 430 kg CO₂/MWh
    • Oil: 900 kg CO₂/MWh
    • Clean sources: 0 kg CO₂/MWh

2024 Import Mix Results:

  • Clean: 70.5% (Nuclear, Hydro, Geothermal, Solar, Wind)
  • Fossil: 22.9% (Coal, Natural Gas, Oil)
  • Unknown: 6.6% (Unspecified + Other)

2025 Treatment: Applied 2024 import ratios to 2025 data (actual dispatch data not yet available)

Classification for Energy Mix:

  • Use CAISO's reported total import MW (unchanged from original)
  • Apply dispatch model ratios to split imports into clean vs. fossil
  • This preserves gross demand consistency while accurately classifying sources

📊 Methodology: Energy Mix V2 (Import Classification)

  • Panel 2 Calendar Trends:

9. Conclusions

California's electricity grid underwent a historic transformation from 2020 to 2025. In just five years:

Major Achievements

  • Clean Energy: Reached 61.6% clean electricity when imports are properly classified (up from ~45% in 2020)
  • Battery Revolution: Deployed 11 GW of battery storage, transforming from novelty to necessity
  • Natural Gas Decline: Cut gas generation by 26.4%, from 8,406 MW to 6,188 MW average daily
  • 100% Clean Hours: Best days achieved 10-11 hours of 100%+ clean generation, with consistent 5+ hour days in spring
  • Negative Prices: Solved the oversupply problem—negative price hours dropped 57% in 2025 after peaking in 2024

Key Lessons

1. Batteries Are Essential

The inflection point came when batteries reached 6-10 GW capacity. This "critical mass" enabled:

  • Absorbing solar oversupply (preventing negative prices)
  • Displacing gas during evening peak (largest emissions reduction)
  • Smoothing the duck curve (reducing ramp requirements)
  • Providing all ancillary services (frequency regulation, reserves)

2. Price Cannibalization Is Real

Battery deployment created a classic "tragedy of the commons":

  • First wave of batteries (2020-2022) earned high revenues from scarce capacity
  • As capacity saturated markets, price spreads collapsed
  • By 2025, ancillary services revenues fell 60-80% from peaks
  • Implication: Future projects need capacity payments, tax credits, or other revenue sources beyond pure arbitrage

3. Import Classification Matters

California's true clean energy progress was understated by treating imports as "unknown." Using merit order dispatch modeling to classify imports reveals:

  • Imports are 70.5% clean (mostly Northwest hydro and Southwest renewables)
  • Accounting for this raises California's clean % by 10+ percentage points
  • Lesson: Grid accounting must trace energy sources across state lines

4. 24/7 Clean Energy Remains Challenging

Despite dramatic progress, achieving 100% clean electricity 24/7/365 requires:

  • Long-duration storage: Current 4-hour batteries can't bridge overnight. Need 8-16 hour systems.
  • Transmission expansion: Access remote wind resources and enable inter-regional balancing
  • Demand flexibility: Shift loads to match generation (EV charging, industrial processes, HVAC)
  • Firm clean resources: Geothermal, next-gen nuclear, or green hydrogen to provide multi-day reliability

Looking Forward

California has proven that rapid grid decarbonization is possible—but the easy gains are behind us. The path to 100% requires:

  • Continued cost declines in storage (targeting $100/kWh for long-duration systems)
  • Policy reforms to reward clean firm capacity, not just energy
  • Market designs that prevent excessive cannibalization while maintaining investment signals
  • Regional coordination to share resources across the Western grid

If California maintains this pace, 80%+ clean electricity is achievable by 2030, with 100% within reach by 2035-2040.
