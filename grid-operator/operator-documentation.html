Grid Operator Game: Complete Technical Documentation

1. Introduction

The Grid Operator Game is an interactive educational simulation that models the challenges facing grid operators in California's evolving power system. As renewable energy penetration increases‚Äîparticularly solar photovoltaic generation‚Äîgrid operators face a complex balancing act: matching electricity supply to demand in real-time while managing the intermittency and variability of renewable resources, maintaining grid frequency stability, minimizing operational costs, and avoiding blackouts.

The game puts the player in the role of a grid operator who must dispatch flexible generation resources (batteries, hydroelectric power, combined-cycle gas turbines, and combustion turbines) to fill the gap between total demand and the renewable energy supply (solar, wind, nuclear, geothermal, and biomass). This gap is referred to as the Net Demand‚Äîthe portion of demand that must be met by dispatchable resources.

The game is built to illustrate the "Duck Curve" phenomenon, where California's midday solar generation creates a deep trough in net demand, followed by a steep ramp in the evening when solar output drops rapidly while demand remains high. This presents significant operational challenges requiring fast-ramping resources and careful energy management.

2. Game Objective and Constraints

The player's objective is to operate the grid for a full 24-hour day (288 five-minute time intervals) while achieving the following goals:

  • Match Net Demand Exactly: Keep the delta (supply minus net demand) as close to zero as possible. Large imbalances cause grid instability.
  • Minimize Operational Costs: Each generation resource has associated operational costs and startup costs. The player must minimize total cost over the 24-hour period.
  • Avoid Grid Outages: Excessive supply-demand imbalances trigger Emergency Alerts or Blackouts, which count against the player.
  • Manage Battery State of Charge (SOC): Ensure the battery does not deplete completely or exceed its capacity.
  • Respect Physical Constraints: Each resource has ramp rates, minimum load requirements, startup times, and capacity limits that must be respected.

3. System Architecture and Simulation Framework

The game is built as a client-side web application using vanilla JavaScript, leveraging the Chart.js library for data visualization. The simulation runs in discrete time steps (ticks), with each tick representing a 5-minute interval in the 24-hour day.

3.1. Time Discretization and Game Loop

  • Time Resolution: 5-minute intervals (288 ticks per day)
  • Game Speed: Configurable from 0.1 to 10.0 seconds per tick (default: 1.0 second per tick)
  • Simulation Clock: The game uses setInterval(gameLoop, gameSpeed * 1000) to advance the simulation at the selected speed
  • Pause/Resume: Players can pause, resume, or restart the simulation at any time

3.2. Data Generation and Forecasting

The game generates synthetic 24-hour profiles for:

  • Total Demand: Based on seasonal load profiles with hourly percentage curves that are interpolated to 5-minute resolution
  • Renewable Generation: Solar and wind capacity factors vary by season and time of day, multiplied by installed capacity to yield actual generation
  • Baseload Resources: Nuclear, geothermal, and biomass are assumed constant throughout the day

6. Game Mechanics and Physics

6.1. Supply-Demand Balance and Grid Frequency

The fundamental equation governing the grid is:

Delta (MW) = Total Supply - Net Demand

Where:

  • Total Supply = Battery + Hydro + CCGT + CT + (Baseload Renewables)
  • Net Demand = Total Demand - Solar - Wind - Nuclear - Geothermal - Biomass

The delta directly affects grid frequency:

  • Delta = 0: Perfect balance, frequency = 60.00 Hz (nominal)
  • Delta > 0 (Oversupply): Frequency rises above 60.00 Hz
  • Delta < 0 (Undersupply): Frequency drops below 60.00 Hz

The frequency calculation uses a simplified linear model:

Frequency (Hz) = 60.00 + (Delta / 10000) √ó K

Where K is a scaling constant that maps MW imbalances to Hz deviations.

The game defines three stability zones based on the delta magnitude:

6.3. Cost Calculation

Operational costs are calculated each tick based on the output of each resource:

  • Battery: Cost = |BatteryMW| √ó $5/MWh √ó (5/60) hours
  • Hydro: Cost = HydroMW √ó $2/MWh √ó (5/60) hours
  • CCGT: Cost = (OnlineUnits √ó $35) + (TotalCCGT_MW √ó $5/500MW √ó (5/60))
  • CT: Cost = (OnlineUnits √ó $65) + (TotalCT_MW √ó $5/200MW √ó (5/60))

The cumulative cost over all 288 ticks gives the total operational cost for the day.

6.4. Battery State of Charge Dynamics

The battery SOC is updated each tick according to the energy balance:

Energy Change (MWh) = -BatteryMW √ó (5/60) hours

SOC Change = Energy Change / 52,000 MWh

New SOC = clamp(Old SOC + SOC Change, 0, 1)

Key points:

  • Discharging (positive MW) decreases SOC
  • Charging (negative MW) increases SOC
  • SOC is clamped to [0, 1] to prevent overcharge/overdischarge
  • Player must manage SOC strategically to ensure battery availability throughout the day

6.5. Unit Dispatch and Power Distribution

For CCGT and CT units, the game implements a unit commitment and economic dispatch model:

  • Pre-Game Commitment: Before the game starts, players can commit units, which come online instantly at maximum capacity and are then dispatched according to the power slider.
  • In-Game Commitment: During gameplay, committing a unit starts a startup timer (60-90 min for CCGT, 10-20 min for CT). The unit transitions to "online" once the timer expires.
  • Power Distribution Algorithm: The distributePowerToUnits() function allocates the target power (from the slider) across all online units, respecting minimum load constraints:
    • Calculate minimum total power required across all online units
    • Clamp target power to [minMW, maxMW]
    • Distribute power proportionally across units, ensuring each unit meets its minimum load
  • Shutdown: Players can shut down units to reduce costs when capacity is not needed. Shutdowns are instantaneous.

7. Seasonal Profiles and Load Variability

The game includes four seasonal profiles (Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter) for both demand and renewable capacity factors:

7.1. Demand Profiles

Each season has a characteristic daily demand pattern defined by hourly percentage values:

  • Summer: High afternoon/evening peak (air conditioning load), peak demand ~30,000 MW
  • Winter: Morning and evening peaks (heating load), moderate midday demand
  • Spring/Fall: Lower overall demand with moderate peaks

The hourly percentages are interpolated to 5-minute resolution using linear interpolation between hourly values.

7.2. Solar Capacity Factors

Solar generation follows a bell curve peaking at midday, with seasonal variations in output:

  • Summer: High solar output (long days, high irradiance), peak CF ~95%
  • Winter: Lower solar output (short days, lower angle), peak CF ~70%
  • Spring/Fall: Moderate solar output

Solar CF is zero from midnight to sunrise and from sunset to midnight, creating the characteristic "duck curve" net demand profile.

7.3. Wind Capacity Factors

Wind generation exhibits less predictable diurnal patterns, with seasonal and hourly variations:

  • Generally higher at night and in the evening
  • Lower during midday hours
  • Seasonal variations in average output

Two semi-circular dial gauges provide real-time feedback:

  • Delta Gauge: Shows the current supply-demand imbalance from -2000 to +2000 MW, with color-coded zones (red for extreme imbalance, yellow for warning, green for stable)
  • Frequency Gauge: Shows the grid frequency from 59 to 61 Hz, with similar color coding

8.3. Game Metrics Panel

Displays key performance indicators:

  • Current Time: Live game clock showing current hour and minute
  • Status Banner: Color-coded status (green = stable, yellow = emergency, red = blackout)

  • Battery SOC: Visual bar chart showing battery state of charge (0-100%)
  • Emergency Alerts and Blackouts: Running counters of grid instability events
  • Current Interval Cost and Total Cost: Economic performance metrics
  • Running Delta Chart: Time series plot of delta values throughout the game, allowing players to see their balancing performance over time

8.4. Control Panels

Players interact with the game through several control panels:

  • Game Controls: Game speed slider (0.1-10.0s per tick), Start/Pause/Resume/Restart buttons, event buttons (Cloud Cover, Demand Spike)
  • Flexible Generation Sliders: Battery power (-13,000 to +13,000 MW), Hydro power (0 to max capacity), CCGT power (min to max based on committed units), CT power (min to max based on committed units)

10. Educational Objectives and Learning Outcomes

The Grid Operator Game is designed to teach players about:

  • Grid Balancing Fundamentals: Real-time matching of supply and demand
  • The Duck Curve: Challenges of high renewable penetration (midday oversupply, evening ramp)
  • Resource Characteristics: Trade-offs between flexibility, cost, and startup time for different generation types
  • Energy Storage Value: The role of batteries in smoothing variability and providing fast response
  • Unit Commitment and Economic Dispatch: Strategic planning of which units to commit and when
  • Operational Costs vs. Reliability: Balancing cost minimization with grid stability
  • Seasonal and Temporal Variability: How demand and renewable output patterns change throughout the year

11. Technical Implementation Details

11.1. Technologies Used

  • HTML5/CSS3: Structure and styling
  • Tailwind CSS: Utility-first CSS framework for responsive design
  • JavaScript (ES6+): Game logic and simulation engine
  • Chart.js: Data visualization library for charts and graphs

11.2. Key Functions and Algorithms

  • generateForecastData(): Generates the 288-point time series for demand and renewable generation based on seasonal profiles
  • gameLoop(): Main simulation loop executed every game tick, updating all state variables and UI elements
  • calculateSupplyDemand(): Computes net demand, total supply, delta, and frequency for the current tick
  • updateSupplyValues(): Updates the chart with current generation values and refreshes UI displays
  • distributePowerToUnits(): Allocates target power across committed units while respecting minimum load constraints
  • processStartupTimers(): Advances startup timers for units in "starting" state and transitions them to "online" when ready
  • updateGauges(): Updates the delta and frequency gauge needle positions based on current grid state

11.3. Data Structures

  • forecastData[]: Array of 288 objects, each containing demand, renewable generation, and time data for one 5-minute interval
  • ccgtUnits[] and ctUnits[]: Arrays of unit objects, each with {state, outputMW, startupTicksRemaining}
  • seasonalProfilesData: Nested object structure containing hourly demand percentages and capacity factors for each season

12. Model Assumptions and Limitations

  • Simplified Frequency Model: The linear frequency response model is a simplification; real grid frequency dynamics involve inertia, governor response, and automatic generation control
  • Perfect Forecasting: Players have perfect knowledge of future demand and renewable generation, which is unrealistic
  • No Transmission Constraints: The model assumes all generation can reach all demand (no grid congestion)
  • Simplified Cost Model: Actual electricity market pricing is complex, involving day-ahead and real-time markets, ancillary services, and locational marginal pricing
  • No Ramp Rate Enforcement: While ramp rates are defined, the simulation does not strictly enforce ramp limits (future enhancement)
  • Deterministic Profiles: Demand and renewable profiles are deterministic; real systems have significant uncertainty

13. Future Enhancements
